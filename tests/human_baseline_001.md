# Human Baseline Answer #001
## Question: "What barriers would a US-licensed doctor face trying to provide telehealth services to a patient in Mexico?"

### Answer (grounded in database records only):

**1. Medical Licensing**
A US medical license is not valid in Mexico. Mexico requires a Cédula Profesional issued by the SEP (Secretaría de Educación Pública) to practice medicine. [Record ID: 362] Board certification through CONACEM is additionally required for specialists and must be renewed every five years. [Record ID: 363] The US Interstate Medical Licensure Compact (IMLC) only covers 42 US states — it does not extend to Mexico or any other country. [Record ID: 374]

**2. No Dedicated Telehealth Law in Mexico**
Mexico has no specific telemedicine law. The General Health Law recognizes digital health and telemedicine as valid forms of care delivery, but there is no dedicated regulatory framework. A NOM (Norma Oficial Mexicana) for telesalud has been drafted but is not yet finalized. [Record ID: 356, 357] This means: it's not explicitly prohibited, but there are no clear rules for a foreign doctor providing telehealth into Mexico.

**3. Controlled Substance Prescribing**
In the US, the DEA/HHS extended telehealth prescribing flexibilities for Schedule II-V controlled substances through Dec 31, 2026. [Record ID: 371] However, the Ryan Haight Act normally requires at least one in-person evaluation before prescribing controlled substances online. [Record ID: 373] Neither of these US flexibilities would apply to prescribing into Mexico, which has its own controlled substances regulations.

**4. Data Privacy Conflict**
US telehealth requires HIPAA-compliant platforms for protecting patient health information. [Record ID: 375] Mexico has its own data privacy law (LFPDPPP) which was updated in 2025 with strengthened consent and accountability requirements. [Record ID: 360] Additionally, Mexico's data protection enforcement body (INAI) was dissolved in March 2025, with responsibilities transferred to the Ministry of Anticorruption. [Record ID: 361] A US doctor would need to comply with BOTH HIPAA and LFPDPPP simultaneously — the requirements may conflict.

**5. Electronic Health Records Compatibility**
Mexico's NOM-024-SSA3-2012 establishes requirements for electronic medical record systems including interoperability standards. [Record ID: 369] US systems may not meet Mexico's specific EHR requirements, creating a technical and regulatory barrier.

**6. Medical Tourism Framework (Not Telehealth)**
Mexico's existing cross-border healthcare is regulated by COFEPRIS for in-person care, with several hospitals holding JCI accreditation. [Record ID: 368] This framework covers medical tourism (patients traveling TO Mexico) — it does not address a foreign doctor providing telehealth INTO Mexico.

### What the database does NOT have (gaps):

- Whether any bilateral agreements between US and Mexico address cross-border telehealth
- COFEPRIS's specific position on receiving telehealth from foreign providers
- Malpractice liability rules when a US doctor treats a Mexican patient remotely
- Whether Mexican insurance (IMSS, IMSS-Bienestar) would cover telehealth from a foreign provider

### Confidence: MODERATE
We have relevant policy records from both countries, but significant gaps exist on the specific question of foreign telehealth INTO Mexico. The absence of a dedicated Mexican telehealth law makes this a grey area — not clearly allowed, not clearly prohibited.
